# The Curse of Unity AI Assist

A Short Horror Story for Future Gauntlet Challengers

There once was a brave Gauntlet AI for America Challenger.

Like many Challengers before her, she stared into the abyss of a looming MVP deadline and confidently declared:

*"I'll just use Unity AI Assist Beta. It will save me time."*

The ancient engineering spirits immediately began laughing.

At first, everything seemed normal.

They opened Unity.

Unity AI Assist opened.

Then Unity AI Assist thought.

And thought.

And thought some more.

Minutes passed.

Entire geological eras passed.

Civilizations rose and fell.

The AI was still thinking.

Finally, after what historians now refer to as *The Long Compile*, Unity AI Assist generated a game.

Technically.

The project contained a player.

The player could move.

Sometimes.

Mostly sideways.

Occasionally through walls.

Frequently into alternate dimensions.

Multiplayer was missing entirely.

The networking code consisted of a comment that simply said:

```csharp
// TODO: networking probably
```

The art was even worse.

The main character looked like a haunted potato.

The environment appeared to have been generated from screenshots of a broken microwave.

The skybox was somehow upside down.

The grass was floating.

The trees were clipping through reality itself.

According to eyewitness accounts, the game was so ugly that even its own mother couldn't stand to look at it.

Several babies reportedly began crying immediately upon seeing screenshots.

One toddler pointed at the screen and whispered:

*"Why?"*

No one had an answer.

Then came the code review.

The MVP reviewer launched the build.

Their monitor emitted a low growling sound.

The reviewer stared at the screen.

The screen stared back.

Their eyes began to burn.

The reviewer closed one eye.

Then the other.

Then both.

For the first time in recorded Gauntlet history, a failing grade appeared.

The reviewer slowly typed:

> "Fail.
>
> We remain hopeful that the challenger can still meet the Final Submission requirements."

The software engineer's blood ran cold.

Every Challenger knew the legend.

They whispered about it during late-night coding sessions.

Some claimed it was merely folklore.

Others knew the truth.

Legend said that any Challenger who failed an assignment would instantly implode and vanish forever from the tech industry.

No LinkedIn.

No GitHub.

No recruiter messages.

No conference talks.

Only an abandoned README and a dusty branch named:

```bash
final-final-v7-actual-final
```

The shadows gathered.

The countdown clock ticked.

The cursed Unity build crashed again.

Then, just when all hope seemed lost...

A distant glow appeared.

Cursor.

Grok.

The twin spirits of emergency software engineering.

They opened a new folder.

A fresh project.

A clean slate.

Within minutes, a new game emerged.

Its name was:

# Orb Chaser

It was simple.

It was functional.

It had real multiplayer.

It met the PRD requirements.

Players could actually play it.

Which immediately made it superior to the cursed Unity build.

An hour later, the Final Submission was complete.

Tests passed.

Requirements passed.

Reviewers smiled.

The curse was broken.

And somewhere, deep inside Unity AI Assist Beta, another loading spinner slowly began to turn...

Waiting for its next victim.

The End.
